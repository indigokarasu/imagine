# COMIC -- Style Prompt

> Two inks on cheap paper, looking straight down

**TL;DR:** Ink line illustration on a flat color ground. Black ink linework -- loose, hand-drawn, variable weight -- over a single dominant background tone (warm peach-orange #F29F62) with one contrasting accent color used sparingly. Bird's-eye perspective. Dense detail filling the entire frame. No shading, no gradients -- form is communicated entirely through line. The look of a two-color risograph or screen print on colored stock.

---

## Rendering Style

Hand-drawn ink illustration. All form is built from linework -- there is no painted fill, no shading, no tonal modeling. Lines vary in weight from fine hairlines (small details, texture marks) to heavier strokes (major structural edges, dense organic forms like hair). The line quality is organic and slightly irregular -- not ruler-straight, not digitally smooth. Curves wobble slightly. Corners don't always close perfectly. Hatching and cross-hatching are used sparingly for localized texture but never for volumetric shading. Stipple dots and small ink splatter marks are scattered across surfaces as texture and visual noise -- they read as grit, age, or imperfection, not as a rendering technique.

## Color System

Two-color printing on a colored ground. The image uses a maximum of three visual tones:

- **Ground color** (#F29F62): warm peach-orange, filling the entire background and all surfaces not otherwise colored. This is not "applied" -- it is the paper stock itself (or reads as such). Everything defaults to this tone unless specifically given the accent color. Skin is not separately colored -- figures are the same tone as their surroundings, differentiated only by linework.
- **Ink black** (#040104 to #150500): all linework, dense fills (hair, deep cavities), text on objects. Warm-leaning black, not blue-black.
- **Accent color** (one per image, used for a single element): a saturated contrasting tone applied as a flat fill to one key area. This accent occupies maybe 15-25% of the image and creates the sole color contrast. It is always flat, never graduated. The specific hue varies per image but is always saturated against the warm ground.

## Perspective & Composition

Directly overhead, bird's-eye view -- looking straight down as if the ceiling or roof has been removed. This flattens depth and turns spaces into diagrams. The composition fills the frame edge to edge with detail -- no negative space, no margins. Every surface is occupied with drawn elements. The density of objects is a defining trait -- these are maximally packed compositions where the entire frame is active.

## Detail Density

Objects within the scene are drawn with obsessive specificity -- enough fidelity to be individually identifiable while remaining loose in execution. Nothing is stylized into abstraction or simplified into icons. The accumulation of many small, specific, recognizable things creates narrative without text. This density is a style characteristic, not a content requirement -- apply it to any subject matter.

## Figure Treatment

Figures are drawn with the same line weight and level of detail as the environment -- they do not pop out or receive special treatment. Hair is the most ink-dense element on a figure. Faces are minimal -- a few lines for features. Bodies are anatomically proportional but not detailed. The figure-ground relationship is deliberately flattened by the shared ground color.

## Mood & Narrative Tone

Intimate and observational. The overhead perspective creates the feeling of witnessing a moment from an impossible angle. The density of detail makes spaces feel real and inhabited. Figures are unself-conscious. The overall tone is quiet, slightly melancholic, domestic -- the ordinary quality of private life held still. There is no story arc, just a frozen moment.
