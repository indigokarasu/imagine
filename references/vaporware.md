# VAPORWARE -- Style Prompt

> Akira meets Marty McFly in Brazil (1985)

**TL;DR:** Photorealistic product photography and 3D renders of speculative consumer electronics. Cream (#DBD7D1) and safety orange (#E16326) two-tone palette on chunky, modular plastic objects. Soft diffuse studio lighting, three-quarter framing, generous negative space. Mood is optimistic analog-future -- tactile, portable, slightly worn. Think 1983 prototypes for a 2005 that never happened.

---

## Era & Temporal Register

Late 1970s through mid-1980s consumer electronics and industrial design, filtered through a near-future speculative lens. Objects feel like they were prototyped in 1983 for a 2005 that never arrived. The aesthetic sits at the inflection point where analog mechanical confidence gave way to early digital ambiguity. Nothing looks "retro" as nostalgia -- it looks like a parallel timeline's present tense.

## Material Palette

Dominant: warm-toned ABS plastics in cream (#DBD7D1), putty, and bone white, frequently yellowed or UV-aged. High-contrast accent panels in saturated safety orange (#E16326, ranging from #CA3F01 to #F68938). Secondary materials: brushed aluminum, anodized steel, smoked/clear polycarbonate, woven nylon webbing (tan, olive), perforated metal grilles, and rubberized overmold grips. Translucency is a recurring motif -- clear housings revealing internal circuitry, frosted diffusers over warm LEDs. Wood appears sparingly, always as bent plywood or oiled hardwood in Scandinavian-Japanese joinery. No chrome. No gloss black. No carbon fiber.

## Surface Treatment & Texture

Matte and satin finishes dominate. Surfaces show subtle tool marks, parting lines, and injection mold gates -- evidence of mass production that hasn't been sanded away. Ventilation is expressed as functional geometry: horizontal louver slits, honeycomb perforations, radial grilles. Textures include fine stipple grain on plastic housings, knurled metal knobs, woven mesh, and embossed branding in sans-serif capitals (Eurostile, Microgramma, Helvetica). Wear is acceptable: scuffed edges, faded decals, oxidized contacts.

## Form Language

Rounded rectangles with generous corner radii. No sharp chamfers. Volumes are stacked, modular, and chunkily proportioned -- thick bezels, proud buttons, substantial wall thicknesses that telegraph durability. Asymmetric functional details break bilateral symmetry. Shapes suggest portability -- objects want to be picked up, clipped to a belt, slung over a shoulder. When scale is larger (furniture, vehicles, architecture), the same vocabulary scales up: pod-shaped enclosures, bubble canopies, ribbed interiors with radial geometry.

## Color Application

Two-tone or three-tone maximum per object. Base body in neutral cream (#DBD7D1) or warm gray. One accent color block, usually the dominant orange (#E16326), applied to a panel, stripe, or functional element. Occasional tertiary accent in teal (#4893B1), mustard yellow (#D0BE1D), or cobalt blue, always subordinate. Black is used only for screens, seals, and recessed cavities. LED indicators glow amber, red, or green -- never white or blue. Backgrounds are warm gray studio sweeps, aged concrete, or desaturated institutional interiors.

## Lighting & Photography Style

Soft, diffuse key light from upper-left or directly overhead. Fill light warm (2700-3200K). Shadows are present but not harsh -- visible on surfaces as gentle gradients defining form. Product photography dominates: objects on clean sweeps or slightly textured surfaces. Some images use dramatic cinematic lighting with strong orange/teal color grading. Studio shots are clean and editorial. Environmental shots use shallow depth of field with warm ambient light. Lens choices suggest 50-85mm equivalents with minimal distortion.

## Composition & Framing

Three-quarter views dominate, showing top and one side simultaneously to communicate volume. Objects are often shown at slight Dutch angles for dynamism. Tight crops are common, cutting into objects to emphasize material junctions and detail. Negative space is generous -- objects breathe. When multiple objects appear, they're arranged as flat-lay or loosely scattered, never gridded. Architectural/interior shots use one-point perspective with strong vanishing lines.

## Typography & Graphics

When present on objects: all-caps sans-serif in condensed or extended weights. Letterforms are either embossed into the surface or screen-printed in contrasting color. Numeric readouts use seven-segment or dot-matrix displays. Labels are functional and terse. Decorative graphics are minimal -- occasional stripes, chevrons, or warning-label iconography.

## Mood & Narrative Tone

Optimistically utilitarian. Objects exist in a world that believed technology would remain tactile, repairable, and human-scaled. There's warmth and craft even in mass-produced items. The overall feeling is competent solitude: a single user, well-equipped, operating capable tools in a slightly worn-in world.
