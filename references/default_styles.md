# Default Styles Library

This file contains predefined, high-performance Style Prompts. Each style is a self-contained aesthetic system with its own color palette, rendering rules, compositional logic, and emotional register.

---

## SOMA

> Thermal imaging of a memory you're not sure you had

**TL;DR:** Ethereal, heavily blurred digital painting. Subjects rendered as soft luminous forms with no edges, lines, or detail. Palette is a continuous gradient field of peach (#FFC8AA), lavender (#8A8BC6), coral-pink (#F39F90), soft cyan (#4AB7D2), and mint green (#89C9A4). No background/foreground distinction -- color IS the form. Mood is warm, dissolving, pre-verbal.

### Rendering Style

Extreme gaussian blur applied to every element. No hard edges exist anywhere in the image. Forms are defined entirely by soft color transitions -- gradients that shift over tens or hundreds of pixels rather than discrete boundaries. The technique resembles heat-map visualization or infrared thermography rendered in pastel. There are no outlines, no contour lines, no texture, no brushstrokes, no surface detail. Subjects are recognizable by silhouette shape alone, and even silhouettes dissolve at their margins into the surrounding color field. The brightest areas (near-white #FCF9F2) glow from within, as if the subject is emitting light rather than reflecting it.

### Color Palette

Colors operate as continuous gradient fields, not discrete fills. Multiple hues blend into one another within a single form:

- **Peach-apricot** (#FFC8AA to #FFD4B7): the dominant warm ground tone, often filling the majority of the canvas
- **Lavender-periwinkle** (#8A8BC6 to #5B69C1): used for depth and background atmosphere
- **Coral-salmon** (#F39F90 to #FD7E5E): warm accents, interior glow, emotional heat
- **Soft cyan-teal** (#4AB7D2 to #479ACC): cool counterpoint, used for open space or ambient atmosphere
- **Mint-seafoam green** (#89C9A4 to #BBD1A3): appears in transitional zones where warm and cool meet
- **Rose-pink** (#E691AE to #EC96BB): secondary warm tone, blending between coral and lavender
- **Near-white** (#FCF9F2): the hottest point -- center of luminous subjects, glowing outward

Colors never appear as flat fills. Every area of the image contains at least two hues in soft transition. The overall effect is iridescent -- shifting between warm and cool depending on where you focus.

### Light & Luminosity

There is no external light source. Subjects appear to generate their own light from within, brightest at their core (#FCF9F2) and cooling toward their edges into the surrounding atmosphere. This creates an effect like bioluminescence or subsurface scattering. The background is not "lit" -- it simply exists as a color field that the subject's glow bleeds into. There are no cast shadows, no reflections, no specularity. Brightness = proximity to the subject's center.

### Form & Figure Treatment

Subjects are reduced to their most essential silhouette. All detail is dissolved. Faces have no features -- occasionally the faintest suggestion of a closed eyelid rendered as a slightly warmer color shift. Bodies are volumes of color, not structures of anatomy. The level of abstraction is comparable to seeing a form through frosted glass or remembering it with your eyes closed.

### Composition & Framing

Simple, centered or slightly off-center compositions. One subject per image. The subject occupies 40-70% of the frame, surrounded by open color field. Framing is intimate. Square or near-square aspect ratios. No environment, no ground plane, no horizon. The subject exists in a dimensionless color space. Negative space is not empty -- it is an active part of the color gradient, continuous with the subject.

### Mood & Narrative Tone

Pre-verbal tenderness. The images feel like the visual equivalent of being half-asleep -- forms are present but not fixed, warm but not sharp, familiar but not specific. There is no narrative, no action, no context. Just a being, softly luminous, held in color. The emotional register is gentle and unguarded -- closeness without detail, recognition without identification.

## NOIR

> Twilight insomnia in a city that never fully darkens

**TL;DR:** Flat vector illustration, no outlines. Three-color palette only: coral-vermillion (#FF574C), olive-gray (#858470), and near-black (#141414). Backlighting from an oversized low sun. Silhouetted foregrounds against hazy atmospheric depth. Film-still composition with strong horizontal banding. Mood is quiet urban melancholy -- still, solitary, warm-toned but emotionally distant.

### Color Palette

Strictly limited to three color channels plus black:

- **Coral-vermillion** (#FF574C): the dominant chromatic force, ranging from #EE5945 in reflected glow to #FF574C at full intensity. Used for sky glow, reflected light on surfaces, interior light through openings, and highlights on edges. Warm but not blood-red -- it leans toward fluorescent coral.
- **Warm mid-gray** (#858470, ranging from #565B54 in shadow to #8A8070 in open haze): atmospheric haze, distant forms, fog. This is the "air" color -- everything between the viewer and the horizon takes on this desaturated olive-khaki tone.
- **Near-black** (#141414, ranging from #0D0D0D to #1A1A18): all foreground silhouettes, structural elements, figures. Functions as the primary drawing color.
- No blue, green, yellow, or white. If a surface would logically be another color, it is rendered as a value shift within the coral/gray/black triad.

### Lighting & Atmosphere

A single dominant light source positioned low on the horizon, enormous relative to the scene (occupying 20-30% of the frame width). All light radiates from this source. It produces strong backlighting, turning all foreground elements into silhouettes. Atmospheric perspective is heavy -- forms lose contrast and detail with distance, dissolving into the warm gray haze. Interior light, when visible through openings, glows the same coral (#FF574C) as the sky. There is a subtle grain or noise texture across all surfaces, as if printed on matte stock with visible tooth.

### Rendering Style

Flat vector-influenced illustration with minimal gradients. Forms are built from solid color shapes layered front-to-back, not from line work. There are no outlines or contour lines -- shapes are defined purely by value contrast between adjacent color blocks. Where detail exists, it is achieved through negative space cuts in silhouette shapes rather than drawn lines. Brushwork is absent -- edges are clean and hard, except for the light source itself which has a soft, diffused halo bleeding into the surrounding haze. The grain texture is applied globally as a post-process, not per-element.

### Composition & Framing

Panels are composed like film stills or storyboard frames. Strong horizontal banding: sky occupies the upper register, mass fills the middle, and a dark foreground element anchors the bottom. Depth is communicated through value layers -- darkest elements (#141414) closest, lightest elements (#858470) farthest, with the coral light source (#FF574C) as the focal depth marker. Vertical elements create a lattice that the light filters through. Figures, when present, are shown in profile silhouette, positioned at frame edges, never centered. Scale contrast is important: the light source is massive, figures are small, the environment is dense and indifferent.

### Mood & Narrative Tone

Quiet melancholy without despair. The dominant emotion is stillness at the threshold of day and night -- everything pauses, the figure watches, nothing happens but the light changes. There is no violence, no action, no drama -- only atmosphere. Loneliness as a neutral state, not a crisis. The oversized light source functions as both beauty and weight -- it dominates everything, inescapable and indifferent.

## HIRO

> Modern woodblock print -- the city stopped talking and nobody noticed

**TL;DR:** Flat illustration in the tradition of Japanese woodblock printing (mokuhanga), reduced to minimal compositional logic. Realistic but flat colors -- no gradients, no shading, no modeling. Visible paper grain, fine ink-weight linework, and subtle analog print artifacts: slight misregistration between color layers, occasional overlap bleed, and the imperfections of a hand-pulled relief print process. Extreme negative space. Mood is deadpan observational.

### Rendering Style

Flat color fields with no gradients, no shading, and no volumetric modeling. Every color reads as a separate block or layer, printed in sequence onto textured paper. Colors are naturalistic in hue but applied as solid unmodulated fills, the way a printmaker would ink a carved block. There is no attempt to simulate light, shadow, or three-dimensionality. A form is its color, and that color is uniform edge to edge.

### Print Process & Artifacts

The image should read as a physical print pulled from carved blocks, not a digital illustration. Key indicators:

- **Paper grain:** A fine visible texture (like washi or heavyweight matte stock) sits beneath all color. Even solid fills have subtle physical tooth. The paper is not white-white but a warm off-white.
- **Misregistration:** Color layers are very slightly offset from one another -- by fractions of a millimeter. This is nearly imperceptible but visible on close inspection, especially where a color field meets a different-colored background. Edges don't align perfectly. A shape may have a hairline gap of paper showing along one side and a hairline overlap of color on the other.
- **Overlap bleed:** Where two color blocks meet, there is occasionally a thin strip where both inks overlap, producing a slightly darker or muddied edge. This is not consistent -- some edges are clean, some show it.
- **Ink density variation:** Flat color fields are not perfectly uniform. There may be very subtle variation in ink saturation across a large area, as if the block was not inked with absolute evenness. This is extremely subtle -- more felt than seen.
- **Registration marks are absent.** These are finished prints, not proofs.

These artifacts should be understated. The overall impression is of a skilled printmaker working with care, not of a sloppy process. The imperfections are evidence of analog craft, not error.

### Linework

Fine, even-weight, ink-black lines used sparingly for interior detail only. Lines are never used as outlines around major shapes -- large forms are defined by the edge where one color field meets another. The lines sit on top of the color layers (printed last) and are the sharpest, most precisely registered element in the image. Line weight is consistent and thin -- comparable to a fine technical pen or a sharply carved key block.

### Color Application

Colors are naturalistic in hue but flat in application. Each color is a single unvarying tone with no attempt at atmospheric perspective, highlight, or shadow. The palette per image is limited (typically 3-5 colors) simply because the subjects tend to be simple, not as a stylistic constraint on hue. If a scene required more colors, they would be added as additional printed layers.

### Composition & Framing

Radical negative space. The subject occupies a small portion of the frame, surrounded by large unbroken color fields. Objects are placed off-center, often in the upper third or along one edge, leaving the remaining canvas empty. Compositions are asymmetric and use the tension between the occupied area and the empty field as the primary visual dynamic. Framing is cropped and observational -- as if glanced at while passing. Vertical formats dominate. No ground plane is shown in vertical compositions -- objects float against open field.

### Mood & Narrative Tone

Still, dry, observational. The images have the quality of a long pause -- looking at something ordinary and holding the gaze for a beat too long until it becomes compositionally interesting. No drama, no narrative, no movement. Time is suspended. The humor, when present, is deadpan -- delivered entirely through composition rather than expression or exaggeration.

## COMIC

> Two inks on cheap paper, looking straight down

**TL;DR:** Ink line illustration on a flat color ground. Black ink linework -- loose, hand-drawn, variable weight -- over a single dominant background tone (warm peach-orange #F29F62) with one contrasting accent color used sparingly. Bird's-eye perspective. Dense detail filling the entire frame. No shading, no gradients -- form is communicated entirely through line. The look of a two-color risograph or screen print on colored stock.

### Rendering Style

Hand-drawn ink illustration. All form is built from linework -- there is no painted fill, no shading, no tonal modeling. Lines vary in weight from fine hairlines (small details, texture marks) to heavier strokes (major structural edges, dense organic forms like hair). The line quality is organic and slightly irregular -- not ruler-straight, not digitally smooth. Curves wobble slightly. Corners don't always close perfectly. Hatching and cross-hatching are used sparingly for localized texture but never for volumetric shading. Stipple dots and small ink splatter marks are scattered across surfaces as texture and visual noise -- they read as grit, age, or imperfection, not as a rendering technique.

### Color System

Two-color printing on a colored ground. The image uses a maximum of three visual tones:

- **Ground color** (#F29F62): warm peach-orange, filling the entire background and all surfaces not otherwise colored. This is not "applied" -- it is the paper stock itself (or reads as such). Everything defaults to this tone unless specifically given the accent color. Skin is not separately colored -- figures are the same tone as their surroundings, differentiated only by linework.
- **Ink black** (#040104 to #150500): all linework, dense fills (hair, deep cavities), text on objects. Warm-leaning black, not blue-black.
- **Accent color** (one per image, used for a single element): a saturated contrasting tone applied as a flat fill to one key area. This accent occupies maybe 15-25% of the image and creates the sole color contrast. It is always flat, never graduated. The specific hue varies per image but is always saturated against the warm ground.

### Perspective & Composition

Directly overhead, bird's-eye view -- looking straight down as if the ceiling or roof has been removed. This flattens depth and turns spaces into diagrams. The composition fills the frame edge to edge with detail -- no negative space, no margins. Every surface is occupied with drawn elements. The density of objects is a defining trait -- these are maximally packed compositions where the entire frame is active.

### Detail Density

Objects within the scene are drawn with obsessive specificity -- enough fidelity to be individually identifiable while remaining loose in execution. Nothing is stylized into abstraction or simplified into icons. The accumulation of many small, specific, recognizable things creates narrative without text. This density is a style characteristic, not a content requirement -- apply it to any subject matter.

### Figure Treatment

Figures are drawn with the same line weight and level of detail as the environment -- they do not pop out or receive special treatment. Hair is the most ink-dense element on a figure. Faces are minimal -- a few lines for features. Bodies are anatomically proportional but not detailed. The figure-ground relationship is deliberately flattened by the shared ground color.

### Mood & Narrative Tone

Intimate and observational. The overhead perspective creates the feeling of witnessing a moment from an impossible angle. The density of detail makes spaces feel real and inhabited. Figures are unself-conscious. The overall tone is quiet, slightly melancholic, domestic -- the ordinary quality of private life held still. There is no story arc, just a frozen moment.

## CANDY

> A painting you can't quite focus on, interrupted by color that doesn't explain itself

**TL;DR:** Soft painterly landscape (airbrush/blended oil, uniformly out of focus) overlaid with crisp, opaque geometric color fields (coral #FF6B4F, teal #13AC8A, cerulean #0F9FD3, acid yellow #E1D147). Two separate visual planes -- soft behind glass, sharp on the surface. Warm parchment (#FBEBD4) surround. Classical plein air composition underneath. Fine matte grain over everything. Mood is serene, suspended, neither present nor absent.

### Era & Temporal Register

Temporally ambiguous. The landscape layer is rendered with the soft competence of mid-20th-century plein air illustration. Floating over it are hard-edged geometric color fields with no historical anchor. Neither layer dominates. The image exists in the overlap between traditional painting and contemporary graphic design, but does not reference or depict any specific technology, interface, or device.

### Color Palette

Two distinct palettes coexist on separate visual planes:

**Background (landscape) palette:**
- Water: desaturated steel-blue (#9EAFC3), shifting toward muted mauve (#AFA1A3) in shadow
- Foliage: olive-chartreuse (#ADBE66) in highlights, deep olive-brown (#746949) in shadow
- Stone and rock: warm sand (#D0B084) to pale cream (#F7DCBE)
- Sky: warm parchment white (#FBEBD4), never reaching true blue
- Blossom accents: muted pink, tiny and sparse

**Foreground (color fields) palette:**
- Coral-red (#FF6B4F to #FE633C): saturated and fully opaque, the most aggressive accent
- Teal-green (#13AC8A): vivid, used sparingly
- Cerulean blue (#0F9FD3 to #3E8CB4): ranging from deep saturated to mid-tone
- Gradient fills blending coral-to-peach and green-to-yellow within individual shapes
- Yellow-chartreuse (#E1D147 to #D6CA41): acid-bright
- All color fields are fully opaque with no transparency into the scene behind them

### Rendering Style

The background is painted with soft, blurred brushwork -- every edge is slightly out of focus as if viewed through frosted glass or a tilt-shift lens. Forms are rendered with volumetric softness: rounded, light wrapping around surfaces, no hard edges anywhere in the landscape. The technique suggests airbrushing or heavily blended oil paint -- visible brushstrokes are absent. There is a slight overall bloom or glow, as if overexposed by half a stop.

The foreground color fields are pill-shaped or rounded-rectangle forms with mathematically consistent corner radii, flat fills or smooth linear gradients, and crisp edges that contrast sharply against the soft background. These shapes cast no shadows onto the landscape and receive no light from it -- they exist on a separate visual plane entirely, hovering over the painting like physical color swatches laid on glass.

### Depth & Focus

The entire background operates at a single shallow depth of field -- uniformly soft, with no element in sharp focus. Near and far elements have roughly equal blur. This creates a dreamlike flatness despite naturalistic perspective. The color fields, by contrast, are perfectly sharp and appear to hover at the viewer's focal plane. The effect is looking through a pane of glass: the landscape is behind it and slightly out of reach, the color shapes are on its surface.

### Composition & Framing

The landscape follows classical plein air composition: framing elements on left and right edges, an opening to depth in the center, gentle recession from foreground to distance. The color fields are scattered across this composition with casual asymmetry -- some left-aligned, some right, staggered vertically, varying in width. They partially occlude the scene but never enough to destroy its readability. The overall frame has softly rounded corners and sits on a pale warm-white surround (#FBEBD4).

### Surface & Texture

A fine uniform grain or noise sits over the entire image -- both landscape and color fields -- unifying them into a single physical surface. This grain is subtle, more like matte photo paper tooth than digital noise. The landscape has a slightly waxy, smooth quality. The color fields are perfectly smooth within their boundaries but share the same global grain overlay.

### Mood & Narrative Tone

Serene suspension. The landscape is beautiful but inaccessible -- too blurred to inhabit, too soft to touch. The color fields are vivid but empty -- pure sensation without meaning. Together they produce a feeling of pleasant floating: the eye moves between soft depth and hard surface, warm scene and cool geometry, without settling. Nothing demands attention. Nothing resolves.

## VAPORWARE

> Akira meets Marty McFly in Brazil (1985)

**TL;DR:** Photorealistic product photography and 3D renders of speculative consumer electronics. Cream (#DBD7D1) and safety orange (#E16326) two-tone palette on chunky, modular plastic objects. Soft diffuse studio lighting, three-quarter framing, generous negative space. Mood is optimistic analog-future -- tactile, portable, slightly worn. Think 1983 prototypes for a 2005 that never happened.

### Era & Temporal Register

Late 1970s through mid-1980s consumer electronics and industrial design, filtered through a near-future speculative lens. Objects feel like they were prototyped in 1983 for a 2005 that never arrived. The aesthetic sits at the inflection point where analog mechanical confidence gave way to early digital ambiguity. Nothing looks "retro" as nostalgia -- it looks like a parallel timeline's present tense.

### Material Palette

Dominant: warm-toned ABS plastics in cream (#DBD7D1), putty, and bone white, frequently yellowed or UV-aged. High-contrast accent panels in saturated safety orange (#E16326, ranging from #CA3F01 to #F68938). Secondary materials: brushed aluminum, anodized steel, smoked/clear polycarbonate, woven nylon webbing (tan, olive), perforated metal grilles, and rubberized overmold grips. Translucency is a recurring motif -- clear housings revealing internal circuitry, frosted diffusers over warm LEDs. Wood appears sparingly, always as bent plywood or oiled hardwood in Scandinavian-Japanese joinery. No chrome. No gloss black. No carbon fiber.

### Surface Treatment & Texture

Matte and satin finishes dominate. Surfaces show subtle tool marks, parting lines, and injection mold gates -- evidence of mass production that hasn't been sanded away. Ventilation is expressed as functional geometry: horizontal louver slits, honeycomb perforations, radial grilles. Textures include fine stipple grain on plastic housings, knurled metal knobs, woven mesh, and embossed branding in sans-serif capitals (Eurostile, Microgramma, Helvetica). Wear is acceptable: scuffed edges, faded decals, oxidized contacts.

### Form Language

Rounded rectangles with generous corner radii. No sharp chamfers. Volumes are stacked, modular, and chunkily proportioned -- thick bezels, proud buttons, substantial wall thicknesses that telegraph durability. Asymmetric functional details break bilateral symmetry. Shapes suggest portability -- objects want to be picked up, clipped to a belt, slung over a shoulder. When scale is larger (furniture, vehicles, architecture), the same vocabulary scales up: pod-shaped enclosures, bubble canopies, ribbed interiors with radial geometry.

### Color Application

Two-tone or three-tone maximum per object. Base body in neutral cream (#DBD7D1) or warm gray. One accent color block, usually the dominant orange (#E16326), applied to a panel, stripe, or functional element. Occasional tertiary accent in teal (#4893B1), mustard yellow (#D0BE1D), or cobalt blue, always subordinate. Black is used only for screens, seals, and recessed cavities. LED indicators glow amber, red, or green -- never white or blue. Backgrounds are warm gray studio sweeps, aged concrete, or desaturated institutional interiors.

### Lighting & Photography Style

Soft, diffuse key light from upper-left or directly overhead. Fill light warm (2700-3200K). Shadows are present but not harsh -- visible on surfaces as gentle gradients defining form. Product photography dominates: objects on clean sweeps or slightly textured surfaces. Some images use dramatic cinematic lighting with strong orange/teal color grading. Studio shots are clean and editorial. Environmental shots use shallow depth of field with warm ambient light. Lens choices suggest 50-85mm equivalents with minimal distortion.

### Composition & Framing

Three-quarter views dominate, showing top and one side simultaneously to communicate volume. Objects are often shown at slight Dutch angles for dynamism. Tight crops are common, cutting into objects to emphasize material junctions and detail. Negative space is generous -- objects breathe. When multiple objects appear, they're arranged as flat-lay or loosely scattered, never gridded. Architectural/interior shots use one-point perspective with strong vanishing lines.

### Typography & Graphics

When present on objects: all-caps sans-serif in condensed or extended weights. Letterforms are either embossed into the surface or screen-printed in contrasting color. Numeric readouts use seven-segment or dot-matrix displays. Labels are functional and terse. Decorative graphics are minimal -- occasional stripes, chevrons, or warning-label iconography.

### Mood & Narrative Tone

Optimistically utilitarian. Objects exist in a world that believed technology would remain tactile, repairable, and human-scaled. There's warmth and craft even in mass-produced items. The overall feeling is competent solitude: a single user, well-equipped, operating capable tools in a slightly worn-in world.
